01.1 Early observations

What the data shows so far

Observations, not recommendations. The trial is still young; the value of these early reads is in pointing to what to watch as more data comes in.

1
Phones are being answered at a reasonable rate. Business-hours answer rates so far run from 71.9% (Newmarket) to 82.5% (Rugby). Rates are calculated against each practice's own opening hours, so they are comparable across sites; with only days of data per site, expect these to move as more weeks roll in.
2
Pre-queue drop sits between 11.5% and 16.8% across sites. That is the share of inbound callers who hang up inside the IVR before reaching the queue. Worth watching over the next few weeks to see whether the range tightens, widens, or shifts as more data accumulates.
3
213 high-value opportunities have already been flagged - calls VoiceStack scored as likely to convert. 129 were rated High intent. These were invisible before the trial; the next-step value depends on whether the team works the list.
4
Missed-call callbacks are now measurable. Response times currently span 3m 31s (Rugby) to 13m 11s (Newark). The metric did not exist before; even before any target is set, having it visible is the change.
5
Call Closing scores vary across sites. Across the group, "Needs Improvement" appears on 29.8% of scored calls - but the spread between sites is wide, so a group-wide reading would be premature. Re-check once each site has a few more weeks of scored calls.

08 Extension load

How answered calls split across the queue

With sequential ringing, calls ring one extension at a time (20 seconds each) before moving on. Reception 1 - the first extension in the chain - therefore picks up first whenever it is free. EARLY OBSERVATION Under sequential ringing, Reception 1 (the first extension in the chain) tends to absorb a larger share of calls - most clearly at the reception-only sites with a single busy front desk. Sites with two genuinely shared reception phones, or a deeper queue, look more balanced. Worth re-checking this read with another two to four weeks of data; if the pattern persists, it becomes a stronger argument for the simultaneous-ringing change those practices have asked about.

Method: counts inbound calls with Call Status “Answered”, after each site’s go-live, with transferred calls (Transfer Index ≠ 1) excluded so each call is credited to the extension that first picked it up. 11 What to watch

Patterns to watch as more data comes in

With 10–18 days of data per site, this is still a short window to commit to specific operational changes. What it is good for is identifying the patterns worth tracking. Another few weeks of data should let each of these resolve into either a real signal or noise.

W1
Does pre-queue drop settle, or stay high?
The share of inbound callers hanging up inside the IVR varies a lot between sites in this short window. If the spread persists once each site has 3–4 weeks of data, that becomes a stronger argument for looking at the IVR set-up. If it evens out, the early variance was likely just noise.
Re-check in 2–4 weeks
W2
Does the Reception 1 imbalance hold?
Under sequential ringing, the first extension in the chain is currently absorbing a larger share of answered calls at some sites. If that holds with more data, it strengthens the case for the simultaneous-ringing change a couple of practices have already asked about - but a couple of weeks is too short to commit on.
Re-check in 2–4 weeks
W3
Are flagged opportunities being worked?
213 high-value opportunities have been flagged so far. The follow-up question is whether the team is acting on the list - calling back, closing the loop, logging outcomes. The data will tell us soon enough; right now we can see only that the list exists.
Track outcomes weekly
W4
Where does callback time settle by site?
Missed-call response time is now measurable per site but is sensitive to a small number of calls in this short window. Once each site has a few more weeks of data, a sensible group target (if there should be one at all) becomes a much easier conversation to have.
Track weekly
